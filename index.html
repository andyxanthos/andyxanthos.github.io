Why is it so hard?

Class Size

  • In large classes, teachers are often unaware of students' individual struggles. (Caldwell, 2006)
  • Students report that large classes do not offer them sufficient time to practice skills they are learning. (Caldwell, 2006)

Motivation

  • Students need to feel like the skills they are learning are applicable to their daily lives. (Dunigan, 2006)
  • It is important that students feel that the basic concepts they are learning are relevant in the big picture. (Bruce, 2004)

Languages

  • Java and C++ are the most commonly taught programming languages. (Lahtinen, 2005)
  • Low-level operations like pointers and pointer arithmetic are especially troublesome for students. (Lahtinen, 2005)

How can we make it easier?

  • Split larger classes into smaller groups for some class sessions

    • During these tutor or teaching assistant lead meetings, students will have a chance to address individual concerns. (Hadjerrouit, 2008)

  • Shift the focus from answering questions to building useful things

    • Encouraging domain-relevant problem solving over rote memorization of language syntax will help to motivate students.

  • Replace Java/C++ with Python

    • Python can be used for anything and is syntactically simple, allowing students to jump right in to writing software.

Prove it!

Process

In order to get the opinions of others, I contacted people who are currently working as programmers and asked them to participate in a ten question survey. One participant was able to speak with me in person, while the remaining participants responded through email. The in-person interview was documented in the form of notes, while the remaining interviews were documented in whole because they were sent by email.

Participants

The participants in this study consist of three people who are currently working as programmers. Importantly, they indicated that their jobs required them to teach others about programming topics and concepts. The participants were initially contacted by an email asking them if they would be interested in participating. It is noteworthy that all of the participants have a friendly or familial relationship with me.

What did they say?

They agreed with me...

  • Class size was a major issue for one participant, but she said small group meetings helped her stay on top of her coursework.
  • One participant said that Python was an ideal choice for beginners. He cited its syntactic simplicity.
  • All of the participants felt that their education did not provide them with specific job skills

And they disagreed with me...

  • Most participants said that C++ and Java are good languages to learn. They cited the fact that skills learned in C++ and Java courses can be more easily applied to different languages.
  • The participants felt that core programming concepts were more important than domain-specific skills.
  • Interestingly, the participants did not mention motivation at all!

    • What can we conclude from this?

    • Python may not be the best choice.

      • While I think Python has its benefits, the professionals I interviewed disagreed. One participant even said that Python hindered his ability to learn other programming languages.
      • The person who said Python would be a good choice works in web development, where Python is a popular choice.

    • Class size is important, but small groups can make it better.

      • One participant confirmed that large class sizes were problematic for her and that small group sessions helped her.

    • Skill building is more important than memorization

      • By building relevant skills, students may feel more prepared to enter the workforce.
